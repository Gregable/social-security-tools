Early and Delayed Spousal Filing

Just like the personal benefit, the spousal benefit can be collected earlier than normal retirement age with a permanently reduced benefit amount. Unlike the personal benefit, delaying the collection date of the spousal benefit until later than normal retirement age will not increase the spousal benefit, only the personal benefit.

There are two rules that apply to the date that {{lowerEarner().name}} can begin taking benefits:

  • {{lowerEarner().name}} is not eligible to begin collecting spousal benefits until {{higherEarner().name}} begins collecting {{higherEarner().name}}'s primary benefit.
  • {{lowerEarner().name}} must elect to collect all of the benefits {{lowerEarner().name}} is eligible for or none the benefits. For example, {{lowerEarner().name}} cannot choose to delay personal benefits but collect spousal benefits if eligible for both benefits. This is known as the deeming rule: you are deemed to be filing for all benefits you are eligible for.

As you can see, if you file for your monthly benefit later in life, the actual benefit is larger. Whether it is better to collect earlier or later in life depends on several factors:

  • If you need income earlier in life, that may be an overriding factor leading you to file for Social Security earlier.
  • If you are concerned about running out of retirement savings at an age where returning to work may no longer be possible, you may choose to file later in order to have a higher monthly income later in life.
  • If you are in good health and feel like you may live longer than average, it may be better to ensure a high monthly benefit later in your life rather than income in your early retirement years.
  • If you are in poor health, it may be the right choice to file earlier because payments stop upon death.

In fact, the Social Security Administration designed the increases from delaying benefits to be financially neutral to the program. The expected payments are the same to the average person regardless of when that person first begins taking his or her benefit. This is done using actuarial tables of average American life expectancies.

Despite many web pages with strong opinions offering the "perfect strategy" for maximizing benefits, this is a very personal decision. The financially optimal filing date depends on the age at which the recipient dies, which is a date no website can predict. Most such sites simply select an average life expectancy and use that, but you are unlikely to live to a precisely average age.
