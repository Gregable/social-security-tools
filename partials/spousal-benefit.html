Special Rule

The month {{spouse.name}} reaches specific ages may seem "off by one" from what you entered. Social Security follows English common law that finds that a person "attains" an age on the day before the birthday. Because {{spouse.name}} was born on the first of the month, this means that {{spouse.name}} attains age {{spouse.exampleAge().age}} on {{spouse.exampleAge().month}} {{spouse.exampleAge().day}}, {{spouse.exampleAge().year}}.

If {{lowerEarner().name}} begins spousal benefits before Normal Retirement Age ({{lowerEarner().normalRetirementDate.monthFullName()}} {{recipient.normalRetirementDate.year()}}), the spousal benefit will be permanently reduced per year you start early by:

  • 6.67% per year if starting AFTER {{lowerEarner().normalRetirement.ageYears - 3}} years and {{lowerEarner().normalRetirement.ageMonths}} months ({{lowerEarner().dateAtYearsOld(lowerEarner().normalRetirement.ageYears - 3).monthFullName()}} {{lowerEarner().dateAtYearsOld(lowerEarner().normalRetirement.ageYears - 3).year()}})
  • 5.00% per year if starting BEFORE {{lowerEarner().normalRetirement.ageYears - 3}} years and {{lowerEarner().normalRetirement.ageMonths}} months ({{lowerEarner().dateAtYearsOld(lowerEarner().normalRetirement.ageYears - 3).monthFullName()}} {{lowerEarner().dateAtYearsOld(lowerEarner().normalRetirement.ageYears - 3).year()}})

The date that {{lowerEarner().name}}'s spousal benefits start is the later of either {{lowerEarner().name}}'s benefits start date or {{higherEarner().name}}'s benefits start date. This can lead to different reduction multipliers for the personal and spousal benefits.

There are two rules that apply to the date that {{lowerEarner().name}} can begin taking benefits:

  • {{lowerEarner().name}} is not eligible to begin collecting spousal benefits until {{higherEarner().name}} begins collecting {{higherEarner().name}}'s primary benefit.
  • {{lowerEarner().name}} must elect to collect all of the benefits {{lowerEarner().name}} is eligible for, or none at all. For example, {{lowerEarner().name}} cannot choose to delay personal benefits but collect spousal benefits if eligible for both benefits. This is known as the deeming rule: you are deemed to be filing for all benefits you are eligible for once one decides to file.

Survivor Benefits

Upon the death of {{higherEarner().name}}, {{lowerEarner().name}} will receive Survivor Benefits. Survivor benefits for {{lowerEarner().name}} will replace {{lowerEarner().name}}'s own benefit, if the Survivor Benefit is larger.

The amount of the Survivor Benefit is typically the amount of {{higherEarner().name}}'s personal benefit at the date of death. This will be reduced if {{lowerEarner().name}} begins collecting the Survivor Benefit before {{lowerEarner().name}}'s Normal Retirement Age ({{lowerEarner().normalRetirementDate.monthFullName()}} {{recipient.normalRetirementDate.year()}}).

More detail on Survivor Benefits can be found at ssa.gov/planners/survivors.

The value of the Survivor Benefit may be something to consider when choosing initial benefit dates for both spouses, but especially {{higherEarner().name}}. Delaying current benefits can have an effect similar to life insurance by providing a higher Survivor Benefit which will persist throughout the surviving spouse's life.
