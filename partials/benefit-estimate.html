Normal Retirement Age

Your primary insurance amount rounded down to the next lower whole dollar is ${{recipient.primaryInsuranceAmountFloored() | number:0}} / month and is the benefit you will earn if you retire at your normal retirement age (NRA). Normal Retirement Age is between 65 and 67, depending on when you were born. You may also see this referred to as the full retirement age (FRA).

For those born in {{birth.year}}, normal retirement age is {{recipient.normalRetirement.ageYears}} years and {{recipient.normalRetirement.ageMonths}} months. This puts your normal retirement date at {{recipient.normalRetirementDate.monthName()}}, {{recipient.normalRetirementDate.year()}}.

Special Rule

The month you reach specific ages may seem "off by one" from what you entered. Social Security follows English common law that finds that a person "attains" an age on the day before the birthday. Because you were born on the first of the month, this means that you attain age {{exampleAge().age}} on {{exampleAge().month}} {{exampleAge().day}}, {{exampleAge().year}}.

If you start collecting benefits at your normal retirement age, your benefit will be the primary insurance amount, rounded down:

  • ${{recipient.primaryInsuranceAmountFloored() | number}} / month
  • ${{recipient.primaryInsuranceAmountFloored() * 12 | number}} / year

Early and Delayed Retirement

You can begin taking benefits as early as 62 years old ({{recipient.dateAtYearsOld(62).monthName()}} {{recipient.dateAtYearsOld(62).year()}}), You can begin taking benefits as early as 62 years and 1 month old ({{recipient.dateAtAge(initAge(62, 1)).monthName()}} {{recipient.dateAtAge(initAge(62, 1)).year()}}), or wait until as late as 70 years old ({{recipient.dateAtYearsOld(70).monthName()}} {{recipient.dateAtYearsOld(70).year()}}), or start any month in between. The longer you wait, the higher your benefit will be.

Special Rule

You may find it oddly specific that the earliest you can actually begin benefits is at age 62 and 1 month. Benefit eligibility is calculated based on the first month that you are a particular age throughout the entire month. For most, this is the month after their birthday.

For example, the first month that you are {{exampleAge().age}} throughout the entire month is {{followingMonth(exampleAge()).month}} {{followingMonth(exampleAge()).year}}.

If you choose to take benefits earlier than normal retirement age ({{recipient.normalRetirementDate.monthName()}} {{recipient.normalRetirementDate.year()}}), your benefit amount will be permanently reduced per year you start early by:

  • 6.67% per year if starting AFTER {{recipient.normalRetirement.ageYears - 3}} years and {{recipient.normalRetirement.ageMonths}} months ( {{recipient.dateAtYearsOld(recipient.normalRetirement.ageYears - 3).monthName()}} {{recipient.dateAtYearsOld(recipient.normalRetirement.ageYears - 3).year()}} )
  • 5.00% per year if starting BEFORE {{recipient.normalRetirement.ageYears - 3}} years and {{recipient.normalRetirement.ageMonths}} months ( {{recipient.dateAtYearsOld(recipient.normalRetirement.ageYears - 3).monthName()}} {{recipient.dateAtYearsOld(recipient.normalRetirement.ageYears - 3).year()}} )

If you choose instead to wait until later than normal retirement age to start your benefits, ({{recipient.normalRetirementDate.monthName()}} {{recipient.normalRetirementDate.year()}}), your amount will be permanently increased by {{recipient.delayIncreaseRate() * 100 | number: 2}}% per year that you delay.

Special Rule

Delayed "credits" are calculated based on the number of months earned in the previous calendar year.

This means that if you begin delayed benefits in a month other than January, your initial benefit will be a little smaller than the full delayed benefit, at first. This difference will disappear beginning in January of the following year, at which point your benefit will increase to the full benefit amount. If you wait until age 70, your delayed credits are applied immediately.
